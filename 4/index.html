A.3 — Warp & Rectify

I implemented inverse warping with two interpolants from scratch. Given a destination pixel \((u,v)\), I map it back with \(x_s \sim H^{-1}[u,v,1]^T\). For nearest, I round \((x_s,y_s)\) to the closest source pixel; for bilinear, I linearly blend the four neighboring source pixels using the fractional parts of \((x_s,y_s)\). For rectification, I click the four corners of a planar object in the image (ordered consistently, e.g., TL→TR→BR→BL) and map them to a perfect square \(\{(0,0),(W-1,0),(W-1,H-1),(0,H-1)\}\). Solving for \(H\) and inverse-warping into a \(W \times H\) canvas removes the perspective skew, yielding a fronto-parallel, metric-aligned view.

A.4 — Mosaic Blending

After estimating homographies between images, I place all views on a common canvas via inverse warping. For blending, each source image contributes two factors per pixel: an alpha coverage mask (1 where the warped image lands, 0 elsewhere) and a smooth distance-to-edge weight that starts near 1 in the interior and decays to 0 at the borders. I compute the weight in the source image as \(w(x,y)=\frac{\min(\text{distance to left/right/top/bottom edges})}{\max(\cdot)}\), then warp this scalar field using the same homography as the RGB channels. On the canvas, I average overlapping pixels using these weights—images dominate near their centers, and seams fade out near the edges—producing a continuous mosaic. For mosaics I used 8 point correspondences per pair to stabilize \(H\).
