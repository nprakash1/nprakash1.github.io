Image Warping & Mosaicing — A.1 to A.4

Overview

Pipeline: A.1 capture projectively related images (rotate in place), A.2 estimate homographies from correspondences, A.3 inverse warp with nearest & bilinear (plus rectification), A.4 feather-blend into mosaics.

A.1 — Shoot the Pictures

I captured three sets of photographs by rotating the camera about a fixed center of projection so that the transformations between them are projective (perspective). I maintained around 50% overlap between the fields of view for easier registration later. The goal was to ensure static scenes, consistent lighting, and enough texture detail for accurate feature correspondence.

A.2 — Recover Homographies

For each image pair, I manually clicked four correspondences and solved the linear system \(A h = b\) using least-squares estimation to obtain the homography \(H\). The homography maps homogeneous coordinates between images, preserving straight lines. Below are example overlays and the computed matrices.

A.3 — Warp & Rectify

I implemented inverse warping with two interpolants from scratch. Given a destination pixel (u,v), I back-project with H⁻¹ to a subpixel source location (xₛ,yₛ). Nearest neighbor rounds (xₛ,yₛ) to the closest integer pixel; bilinear mixes the four neighbors using the fractional offsets. For rectification, I click four ordered corners on a planar object and map them to a perfect W×H rectangle. Solving for H and inverse-warping into that canvas removes the perspective skew, yielding a fronto-parallel view.

A.4 — Mosaic Blending

After estimating homographies between images, I warp each image into a shared canvas (inverse warping). To hide seams, I don’t overwrite pixels; instead each image contributes a smooth weight that’s high near its center and tapers to zero at the edges (computed by distance to the image borders). I warp this scalar weight with the same homography as the RGB and multiply by an alpha mask that indicates where the warped image actually lands. Finally I average the colors using these weights so regions where two images overlap blend smoothly rather than cutting sharply. For mosaics I used 8 point correspondences per pair for added stability.
