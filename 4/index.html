Image Warping & Mosaicing — A.1 to A.4

Overview

Pipeline: A.1 capture projectively related images (rotate in place), A.2 estimate homographies from correspondences, A.3 inverse warp with nearest & bilinear (plus rectification), A.4 feather-blend into mosaics.

A.1 — Shoot the Pictures (Bigger Pairs)

I captured multiple overlapping views by rotating the camera about a fixed center of projection to ensure the transforms are projective. I avoided strong lens distortion and tried to keep content planar where possible. Each pair below has ~40–70% overlap to make registration feasible. These are raw pairs straight from the camera (JPGs for this section).

A.2 — Recover Homographies (Bigger Visuals)

For each pair, I manually clicked four correspondences (the minimum) and formed the linear system \(A\,h=b\) for the 8 unknowns of the homography (with bottom-right fixed to 1). I solved it in least squares and reported \(H\) that maps image 1 → image 2. The displayed system rows are the exact equations produced by my clicks. Because I used only 4 points here, the solution is more sensitive to click noise; later, for mosaics, I increase to 8 points for robustness.

A.3 — Warp & Rectify (Exactly 3 Images)

I implemented inverse warping with both nearest neighbor and bilinear interpolation from scratch. To verify the pipeline, I performed rectification by clicking the four corners of a planar object and mapping them to a perfect square in the destination. For this report, I present two “before” inputs and a single combined “after” PNG that shows both rectified results side-by-side. Bilinear produces cleaner results with fewer aliasing artifacts, while nearest neighbor is useful for quick previews.

A.4 — Mosaic Blending

For mosaicing, I registered images into a single canvas and blended them with distance-to-edge feathering multiplied by alpha coverage. Instead of overwriting pixels, each warped image contributes a weight that ramps down near its borders; this reduces seam visibility and hard edges. I used my manual correspondences and stepped up to 8 points per pair here to make the homographies more stable and the overlap alignment tighter. The results below show three different mosaics rendered as PNGs.

Notes

  • Planarity of clicked points strongly affects alignment quality.
  • Nearest neighbor is fast; bilinear reduces aliasing/ghosting.
  • Feathering minimizes seams; Laplacian pyramid could further reduce high-frequency ghosting if needed.
